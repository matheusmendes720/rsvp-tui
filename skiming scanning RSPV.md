# skiming scanning RSPV

Skimming, scanning, and RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation) are key speed reading strategies and techniques, each serving unique purposes in reading and information processing.[^1_1][^1_2][^1_3]

### Skimming

Skimming is a rapid reading method used to grasp the general idea or main themes of a text without focusing on details. It involves quickly reading horizontally through the text—typically the headings, subheadings, and topic sentences—to understand the structure, overall topic, and usefulness of the content. Skimming is ideal when previewing material or when seeking to determine if a text warrants further reading.[^1_4][^1_5][^1_2][^1_6][^1_7][^1_8]

### Scanning

Scanning is a targeted reading technique focused on finding specific information, such as keywords, dates, numbers, or names, within a text. Rather than reading every word, the eyes rapidly move vertically or diagonally to locate the desired details. Scanning is especially effective for research tasks or when searching for answers to specific questions.[^1_5][^1_9][^1_6][^1_10][^1_11][^1_12]

### RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation)

RSVP is a speed reading and research tool where text is shown one word or phrase at a time in a fixed spot on a screen or display device. This minimizes eye movement (saccades) and allows for significantly faster reading speeds while aiming to retain comprehension. RSVP software or tools progressively display words at controlled rates (like 400 words per minute), challenging readers to process and comprehend content efficiently. RSVP is used both in reading research and in commercial speed reading applications.[^1_3][^1_13][^1_14][^1_1]

### Summary Table

| Strategy | Purpose | Technique | Use Case |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Skimming | Get the gist/main ideas | Fast reading across text; focusing on headers, topic sentences | Previewing, reviewing, general understanding [^1_5][^1_4][^1_6] |
| Scanning | Find specific information | Eyes move quickly to search for keywords or data | Answer checking, research, locating facts [^1_5][^1_9][^1_6] |
| RSVP | Increase reading speed | Words shown one at a time in place; minimal eye movement | Speed reading apps, scientific reading studies [^1_1][^1_3][^1_13] |

# best and most usefull github project RSPV , speed reading .md files!

For RSVP-style speed reading of `.md` files, the two most useful open‑source projects on GitHub right now are **`rsvp-term`** and **`srit (Speed Read It!)`**, both terminal-based and built specifically to handle Markdown.[^2_1][^2_2]

## rsvp-term (TUI for Markdown \& EPUB)

- `Mootikins/rsvp-term` is a **terminal UI** app designed explicitly for RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation) speed reading of **Markdown and EPUB files**, so you can point it directly at `.md` documents without pre‑converting them.[^2_1]
- Because it runs in the terminal and is focused on Markdown/EPUB, it fits really well into a CLI/vim/Neovim workflow where your notes and docs are already in `.md`.[^2_1]


## srit – Speed Read It! (CLI for Markdown, PDF, Word)

- `yurug/srit` is a command‑line RSVP reader that can speed‑read “any text” and explicitly supports **text, Markdown, PDF, and Word documents (.doc/.docx)** as inputs.[^2_2]
- If you want one tool that can handle `.md` plus more “document” formats (PDF, Word) from the terminal, this is a strong option.[^2_2]


## Other RSVP tools if you’re okay converting Markdown

If you are willing to convert Markdown to plain text or another format first, there are more RSVP readers you can experiment with:

- `trakBan/speedreader` is a Python CLI speed‑reading tool that lets you configure words per minute, aimed at making you “read faster” from the terminal, though it’s not specialized for Markdown.[^2_3]
- Various web‑based RSVP readers (for example, Svelte‑based “RSVP Reader” apps and similar projects under the `rsvp` GitHub topic) let you paste or load text and read it at 100–900+ WPM or more, but they generally don’t have first‑class `.md` file handling.[^2_4][^2_5][^2_6][^2_7]

If you want something that “just works” with `.md` in a terminal workflow, start with **`rsvp-term`**, then try **`srit`** if you also care about PDFs/Word.[^2_2][^2_1]
